             # AN AIRLINE LOYALTY PROGRAM ANALYSIS IN CANADA
This report analyzes customer flight activity and loyalty program engagement across Canada  between 2017 and 2018, using historical loyalty data from 2012 to 2018.  The analysis combines  insights from flight behavior, customer demographics, engagement patterns, and geographic  performance to provide a comprehensive view of customer value and program effectiveness. 

# 1. INTRODUCTION 
Airline loyalty programs are designed to encourage repeat travel, increase customer lifetime 
value, and strengthen brand loyalty. Understanding how customers interact with the program—through flights taken, points earned, and points redeemed—is critical for evaluating its effectiveness. 
This analysis seeks to answer the following business questions: 
• How active are loyalty program members over time? 
• Which customer segments generate the highest value? 
• How efficiently are loyalty points being redeemed?

# 2. KEY FINDINGS
• A total of 392,936 flights were recorded compared to 16,737 loyalty members, indicating a large gap between flight activity and loyalty enrollment. 

• Customers accumulated 796.55 million points but redeemed only 12 million, showing low redemption rates. 

• Total distance traveled reached approximately 763 million miles, reflecting high customer activity. 

• July recorded the highest flight activity (64,248 total flights), with 37,936 flights in July 2018 alone. 

• Star card holders were the most active, recording 231,051 flights and 45.73% of total points redeemed.

• Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) is nearly identical across genders, with males slightly higher than females. 

• Standard enrollment accounts for 94.2% of cancellations, significantly higher than promotional enrollment (5.8%). 

• Loyalty program membership grew steadily from 1,686 in 2012 to 3,010 in 2018. 

• Majority of customers (14,670) remained active, though 2018 recorded the highest cancellations (645). 

• Customers with $0 recorded salaries accumulated the highest points, indicating possible data quality issues. 

• Females (8,410) slightly outnumber males (8,327) in the loyalty program. 

• Married individuals with bachelor’s degrees (7,112) form the largest customer segment. 

• Ontario recorded the highest CLV ($42.77M) and largest customer base.

• Toronto had the highest points redemption ($443,248), while Charlottetown had the lowest ($8,690). 

• New Brunswick had the highest average salary ($63,054.02), while Newfoundland had the lowest ($56,064.13). 

# 3. CONCLUSION
The analysis reveals that customer engagement within the airline loyalty program is strong but unevenly utilized. While customers demonstrate high levels of activity—evidenced by significant flight volumes, miles traveled, and points accumulated—points redemption remains low, indicating untapped value within the loyalty program. 
Seasonal trends, particularly the mid-year travel peak in July, highlight predictable patterns that can be leveraged for targeted marketing and promotional campaigns. The dominance of Star card holders and the concentration of activity in Ontario and major cities like Toronto suggest that engagement is driven by specific customer segments and geographic regions. Demographic insights show that customer value is consistent across genders, while education 
and marital status play a role in customer distribution. However, the high concentration of cancellations within standard enrollment types and anomalies such as high points accumulation among zero-salary customers indicate areas requiring strategic attention and data validation. 
Overall, the findings emphasize the need for improved loyalty program optimization, particularly in increasing points redemption, enhancing customer retention strategies, and addressing data quality issues. Leveraging these insights will enable more effective decision-making, improved customer experience, and greater value generation from the loyalty program.
